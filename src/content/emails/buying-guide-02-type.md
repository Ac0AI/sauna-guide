# Which Sauna Is Right for You?

Hey,

Yesterday we covered what a home sauna actually costs. Today we'll figure out which type you should get. The answer isn't the same for everyone, and anyone who tells you there's one "best" option is probably selling it.

Let's work through this.

## The two big decisions

Before you compare brands or read reviews, you need to answer two questions:

1. **Infrared or traditional?**
2. **Indoor or outdoor?**

Everything else follows from there.

## Infrared vs. traditional

This is where most of the confusion lives. Here's the honest difference.

**Infrared saunas** heat your body directly with infrared panels. The air stays relatively cool (120-150F). They plug into a regular outlet, heat up in 15-20 minutes, and fit in a spare room or large closet. They're the easiest path to daily sauna use.

**Traditional saunas** heat the air with a heater and rocks. The room gets hot (150-200F). You can throw water on the rocks for steam (loyly). They need 240V electrical, proper ventilation, and 30-45 minutes to heat up. This is the experience most people picture when they think "sauna."

**Our take**: If you've never used a sauna regularly, both will feel amazing. If you've been to Finland or spent time in a real sauna, infrared will feel like a compromise. Neither is wrong. It depends on what you want.

Here's a quick decision filter:

- **Go infrared if**: You want minimal installation, you rent your home, you want to use it daily with no warmup time, or your budget is under $4,000.
- **Go traditional if**: You want the full experience (steam, ritual, the whole thing), you own your home, you're okay with a 30-minute warmup, and you can budget $6,000+.

We wrote a deep comparison here: [Infrared vs. Traditional Sauna: The Complete Guide](/guides/infrared-vs-traditional-sauna)

## Indoor vs. outdoor

This one matters more than most people think.

**Indoor saunas** are convenient. They're always at room temperature to start, so they heat up faster. No trudging through snow. But they need proper ventilation, moisture management, and a dedicated space. A spare bathroom or basement corner works well. A bedroom closet does not (we've seen people try).

**Outdoor saunas** are the most popular choice for traditional builds. Barrel saunas, cabin-style kits, even converted sheds. Ventilation is simpler because you're outside. The downside: they take longer to heat in winter, and the walk from your house to the sauna in January will test your commitment.

**The usage question**: Here's what nobody tells you. The #1 predictor of whether you'll actually use your sauna regularly is how easy it is to get to. A sauna 50 feet from your back door gets used. A sauna in a detached building at the bottom of your yard does not. Think about the path from your daily routine to the sauna door.

## DIY build vs. pre-built kit

**Pre-built kits** (barrel saunas, modular rooms) arrive ready to assemble. Most take a weekend with two people. The wood, the heater, the benches are all included. You're paying a premium for convenience, but you're also getting something that's been engineered to work together.

**DIY builds** save 30-50% on materials but require carpentry skills, knowledge of vapor barriers, and an understanding of sauna ventilation. If you've never built anything, this is not the place to start. If you've done bathroom remodels or deck builds, a sauna is very doable.

**Our take**: Unless you're an experienced builder, get a kit for your first sauna. You can always build a custom one later when you know exactly what you want. We've seen too many DIY builds with bad ventilation or wrong wood choices that become expensive lessons.

## The decision matrix

Here's how we'd sort it:

| You want... | Get this |
|-------------|----------|
| Easiest possible setup, daily use | Indoor infrared ($2,000-$4,000) |
| Real sauna feel, reasonable budget | Outdoor barrel sauna ($6,000-$10,000 total) |
| The full experience, space and budget available | Custom outdoor build ($12,000-$25,000) |
| Testing it out before committing | Indoor infrared or find a local sauna first |
