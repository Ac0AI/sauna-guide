Sauna Guide

Before You Begin

This isn't a set of rules. It's an invitation to slow down.

The Finns have been doing this for 2,000 years without timers, heart rate monitors, or optimization protocols. They simply step into the heat, stay until it feels right, and repeat.

What follows is a framework — tested by tradition, refined by modern understanding. Take what serves you. Leave what doesn't.

1. Before the Heat

Hydration

Drink water throughout the day. Not a gallon right before — just steady, consistent hydration. You'll lose 300-500ml of sweat per session. Your body knows this is coming.

Timing

  • Morning sessions: energizing, sets the tone
  • Evening sessions: unwinding, better sleep
  • After exercise: enhanced recovery, muscle relaxation
  • Before bed: leave 1-2 hours for your body to cool

What to avoid

  • Heavy meals within 2 hours
  • Alcohol (despite what you've seen in movies)
  • Intense exercise immediately before

What to bring

  • Water (room temperature, not ice cold)
  • A towel to sit on
  • Nothing else. That's the point.

2. Temperature & Duration

There is no "optimal" temperature. There's only what your body tells you.

Traditional Finnish sauna

  • Temperature: 70-100°C (158-212°F)
  • Duration: 10-20 minutes per round
  • Humidity: Low to moderate

Infrared sauna

  • Temperature: 45-65°C (113-149°F)
  • Duration: 20-45 minutes
  • Penetration: Deeper tissue warming at lower temperatures

Steam room

  • Temperature: 40-50°C (104-122°F)
  • Humidity: 100%
  • Duration: 10-15 minutes

By Intention

Goal Temperature Duration Rounds
Relaxation 70-80°C 15-20 min 2-3
Deep sweat 80-90°C 10-15 min 3-4
Recovery 70-80°C 10-15 min 2-3
Sleep prep 65-75°C 15-20 min 2
The Finnish rule: If you're watching the clock, you're not present. Enter the heat. When your body says "enough," step out.

3. The Round Structure

This is the rhythm. Heat, cool, rest. Repeat.

Round 1: Awakening

  • Enter the sauna
  • Sit or lie down (upper benches are hotter)
  • Focus on your breath
  • 10-15 minutes, or until you feel ready to cool

Cooling

  • Cold shower, plunge pool, or fresh air
  • Start with cool, progress to cold over time
  • 30 seconds to 2 minutes
  • The contrast is where the magic happens

Rest

  • Sit or lie somewhere comfortable
  • Room temperature
  • 5-10 minutes
  • This is when your body recalibrates

Round 2 & 3: Deepening

  • Return to the heat
  • You'll tolerate more now
  • The mind quiets
  • Same pattern: heat → cool → rest

Round 4: Integration (optional)

  • Final, shorter round
  • Often the most meditative
  • End with cooling and longer rest

Total session: 60-90 minutes, including rest periods.

4. Breathing in the Heat

Your breath is your anchor.

Simple practice

  • Inhale slowly through the nose (4 counts)
  • Hold briefly (2 counts)
  • Exhale slowly through the mouth (6 counts)
  • The longer exhale activates relaxation

When the heat intensifies

  • Keep breathing slow and steady
  • Shallow, rapid breathing signals panic
  • Deep breaths tell your body you're safe

Advanced: Löyly breathing

When the steam rises (löyly — the Finnish word for "spirit"):

  • Breathe in the wave of heat
  • Let it fill your lungs
  • Exhale tension
  • The steam carries more than moisture

5. After the Heat

What you do post-sauna matters.

Immediately after

  • Shower to rinse sweat
  • Rest for 10-20 minutes
  • Let your heart rate normalize

Rehydration

  • Water with electrolytes
  • Herbal tea
  • Light snack if hungry
  • Avoid alcohol for at least 30 minutes

The glow

  • Your skin will be flushed
  • Your mind will be quiet
  • This feeling lasts 2-4 hours
  • Protect it. Don't immediately dive into screens or stress.

Sleep

If you sauna in the evening:

  • Your core temperature will drop as you cool
  • This drop signals sleepiness
  • Many report the best sleep of their lives

6. Contrast Therapy

The cold is not punishment. It's the other half.

The practice

Heat (sauna) → Cold (plunge, shower, snow) → Rest

The contrast triggers circulation, endorphin release, and deep relaxation.

Cold options (warmest to coldest)

  • Cool shower
  • Cold shower
  • Cold plunge (10-15°C / 50-59°F)
  • Ice bath (2-5°C / 35-41°F)
  • Snow or frozen lake (for the brave)

Building tolerance

  • Start with cool showers
  • Increase cold exposure gradually
  • Never force it
  • The goal is invigoration, not suffering

The physiology

  • Blood vessels constrict (cold) then dilate (heat)
  • This "pumping" enhances circulation
  • Inflammation decreases
  • Mood elevates

7. The Science

For those who want to know why it works.

Cardiovascular

  • Heart rate increases to 100-150 bpm (similar to moderate exercise)
  • Blood vessels dilate, improving circulation
  • Regular use associated with reduced cardiovascular disease risk

Laukkanen et al., JAMA Internal Medicine, 2015

Heat Shock Proteins

  • Activated at temperatures above 39°C
  • Help repair damaged proteins
  • May slow cellular aging

Hooper, 1999; Kregel, 2002

Endorphins & Mood

  • Heat stress triggers endorphin release
  • Comparable to "runner's high"
  • Regular sauna linked to reduced depression symptoms

Kunutsor et al., 2018

Sleep

  • Post-sauna temperature drop mimics natural sleep onset
  • Growth hormone increases during deep sauna sessions
  • Sleep quality improves with regular practice

Hussain & Cohen, 2018

Inflammation

  • Heat therapy reduces inflammatory markers
  • May help with chronic pain conditions
  • Accelerates muscle recovery after exercise

Mero et al., 2015

The best research comes from Finland, where sauna is cultural infrastructure, not wellness trend.

8. Building Your Practice

Start where you are.

Week 1-2: Familiarization

  • 1-2 sessions per week
  • Single rounds (15-20 minutes)
  • Lower temperatures if available
  • Cool showers after

Week 3-4: Building

  • 2-3 sessions per week
  • 2 rounds per session
  • Introduce longer cooling
  • Add the rest period

Month 2+: Deepening

  • 3-4 sessions per week
  • 3-4 rounds per session
  • Cold plunge if accessible
  • The practice becomes ritual
Consistency over intensity: 4 weekly sessions beat 1 extreme session. This is a practice, not an event. The benefits compound over months and years.

A Final Word

The sauna is not a place to achieve.
It is a place to be.

No notifications. No optimization. No performance.

Just heat. Silence. Breath.

"The sauna is a poor man's pharmacy."
— Finnish proverb
"In the sauna, one must conduct oneself as one would in church."
— Finnish saying

Not because it's sacred.
Because in there, finally, you can stop.
